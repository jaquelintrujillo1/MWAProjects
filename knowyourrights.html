Everyone in the U.S has rights guaranteed under the U.S Constitution, regardless of immigration status. Understanding these rights will help you advocate and respond correctly to any police or immigration enforcement, for yourself and for others.

Your Rights Inside of Your Home

The most important things to remember when interacting with law or immigration enforcement are:

  • Stay calm
  • Stay silent
  • Record details and names
  • Do NOT run away (it gives the officers suspicion that you are guilty of something
  • Ask to speak to your lawyer before signing any documents or answering any questions
  • DO NOT lie

    • If ICE knocks on your door, you have the right to not open the door. ICE can only enter if they have a valid judicial search or arrest warrant with your correct name and/or address, signed by a judge.

    • Ask the officer to slip the warrant under the door or hold it up to your front window so you can examine it.
    • ICE will have a warrant of deportation or removal
    • If it is signed by an immigration official and not a judge then,that does not give them permission to enter your home.
    • They can only enter your home if you give permission, if they do not have warrant signed by a judge, you should NOT open the door.

    officers might refuse to show their warrant, if they do, you have the right to say, "I am exercising my constitiutional right and will not open my door until you demonstrate a valid warrant."

    A warrant must have:

    • Signed by a judge, not an immigration official
    • Contain your address to be searched
    • Describe the area to be searched

    ICE cannot force a landlord to open your home without a valid warrant

    If an ICE offical enters your home without permission

    • Stay calm and call an attorney
    • Say "I have the right to remain silent."
    • DO NOt sign anything
    • Record incident, officer's badge number, agency and full name.
    • If you are asked to stop recording, do so, because it might make the situation worse.
    • If you have children that are US citizens, tell the officers, because they might not detain you. DO NOT tell the officers if your children are undocumented, because they might be detained as well.

    Your Rights Outside of Your Home

    There is more pressure when encountering an immigration officers outside of your home. Make sure you know your rights!

    ICE is allowed to arrest you in public, if they have a warrant with your name on it.

    • Remember to say calm and not to run.

    If an officer talks to you say, “Am I being arrested or detained?” and says no then ask, “Am I free to leave?” If they say yes then walk away calmly.

    Workplace

    Make a plan with your coworkers to:

    • Stay calm
    • Not run
    • Stay silent

    Employers have the right to not let anyone into the building without a warrant. You should let your employer know this because they will be able to understand their rights and protect their employees.

    While Driving

    If stopped while driving

    • Show the police your driver's license
    • Show your car registartion and proof of insurance
    • If they ask to search your car, you can refuse.
    • If they police believes your car contains evidence of a crime, your car can be searched without consent.

    It is illegal for law enforement to perfrom searches based on your religion, race, national origin, gender, ethnicity, or political beliefs.

    Law enforcement officers at an airport have the authority to seach your bags and ask about your citizenship. They will not if you are just flying domestically.

    It is important not to fly with a valid ID. For example, state-issued driver's lisence, state ID, passport from your country of origin, or DACA EAD card.

    Rights of Lawful Permanent Residents

    • Permaent residents have many rights under the Constitution and existing immigration law.
    • The only person that can take away your permanent residence is a judge.

    Permanent Reidence have to right to

    • Work in the United States
    • eligible for public benefits
    • The right to travel in the United States and other countries

    If you are convicted of a crime, your permanent residence status can be taken away.
