Timeline of Immigration Laws from 2017-2019

Feburary 15, 2019

Trump signs bill to fund parts of the government and border barrier; declares state of emergency

President Donald Trump signed a $328 billion spending bill that included $1.375 billion in funding for barriers on the southern border. He had requested more for wall funding and because he did not receieve the amount requested, he declared a state of emergency on the southern border and directed $8.1 billion to build a border wall. Trump said, “It’s a great thing to do because we have an invasion of drugs, invasion of gangs, invasion of people.” His actions have been criticed by House Speaker Nancy Pelosi and Senate Minority Leader Chuch Schumer, saying, "The president's unlawful declaration over a rissi that does not exsist does great violence to our Constitution and makes America less safe. The president is not above the law. The Congress cannot let the president shred the Constitution."

February 3, 2019

Trump administration announces more troops headed to southern border

The U.S. Department of Defense announced that 3,700 members of the military would be sent to the southern border. They were sent there to assist Customs and Border Protection. Before the deployment, there were nearly 650 troops at the border.President Trump innitially wanted to send troops to the board on April 4, 2018 Trump says that they were deployed to stop "a drastic surge of illegal activity on the southern border."

January 19, 2019

Trump releases plan to secure border and end partial shutdown

President Trump created a plan to secure the southern boarder and end the government's partial shutdown

Trump's plain included:

  • $5.7 billion to fund a steel barrier system
  • $805 million for technology, canines, and personnel to prevent drugs and weapons from being brought into the country
  • $800 million dollars in humanitarian assistance, medical support, and new temporary housing
  • $782 million to hire an additional 2,750 border agents, law enforcement officers, and staff
  • $563 million for the immigration court system, including hiring 75 new immigration judge teams
  • Three years of provisional status for DACA and certain Temporary Protected Status (TPS) recipients, which would protect them from deportation.

January 8, 2019

Democratic Leaders reject Trump's border barrier address

In a televised address on January 8, 2019, President Trump President said there was a crisis the southern boarder and wanted Congress to give $5.7 billion to build a wall or steel barrier. He said, “At the request of Democrats, it will be a steel barrier rather than a concrete wall. This barrier is absolutely critical to border security. It’s also what our professionals at the border want and need.”

December 21, 2018

Trump shut downs the government for border wall funding

The government shut down was in response to funding for the border wall not included in a continuing resolution (CR) to fund the government. On December 21, 2018, President Trump made the address announcing that there would be a government shut down until he gets funding for the border wall. After, $5 billion was passed for funding for a border wall.

October 30, 2018

Trump proposes ending birthright citizenship

President Trump said in an inter view that he would sign an executive order ending birthright citizenship to children of noncitizens. Trump said, "We're the only country in the world where a person comes in and has a baby, and the baby is essentially a citizen of the United States ... with all of those benefits. It's ridiculous. It's ridiculous."

Trump signs an executive order addressing the separation of children from parents crossing the border illegally

President Trump signed an executive order to keep immigrating detained families together. Another part of it is that the U.S.Department of Defense had to assist in finding the families housing when detention centers are full. On May 7, 2018, the paretns would be seperated from their chidlren until there was a result to their case. Trump said, "We’re going to keep families together but we still have to maintain toughness or our country will be overrun by people, by crime, by all of the things that we don’t stand for and that we don’t want."

June 11, 2018

Sessions says individuals who are victims of private crime not eligible for asylum

On June 11, 2018, Attorney General Jeff Sessions announced that people who are victims of personal crime are not elegable for asylum in the United States. U. S. Sessions said, "Our nation’s immigration laws provide for asylum to be granted to individuals who have been persecuted, or who have a well-founded fear of persecution, on account of their membership in a ‘particular social group,’ but most victims of personal crimes do not fit this definition—no matter how vile and reprehensible the crime perpetrated against them."

May 1, 2018

Texas and six other states file lawsuit to end DACA

On May 1, 2018, Texas and six other states filed a lawsuit because they believed that the continuation of the DACA program was unlawful. They argued that the program required them to spend more money on healthcare, law enforcement, and education. Also, the program increased competition for jobs.
